# Short Podcast Script

**Title:** Beyond Dashboards: Can AI Reduce Global Health Inequities?

[Opening music fades in]

Hello, and welcome.

My name is Rayton Kiwelu. I am a database developer and digital health professional, and my work focuses on health systems in resource-limited settings. I work with large health datasets, system integration, and digital tools that support care and treatment programs.

Through that work, I have seen two sides of digital health. I have seen technology improve services, strengthen reporting, and support better decisions. But I have also seen poorly designed systems create more work, more confusion, and sometimes more inequality.

That is why I want to talk about artificial intelligence, or AI, and one important question:

How can we use AI in healthcare without making global health inequities worse?

[Pause]

AI is already becoming part of healthcare around the world. It is being used to support diagnosis, analyze medical images, predict outbreaks, improve supply chains, and help health workers make faster decisions.

That potential is real.

But so is the risk.

Most AI tools are developed and trained in high-income settings, using data from hospitals with strong infrastructure, stable electricity, reliable internet, and well-connected digital systems.

That is not the reality in many low- and middle-income countries.

In many places, health workers are dealing with limited resources, high patient volumes, unstable connectivity, and multiple systems that do not communicate well with each other. So if an AI tool is built for one environment and then used in a very different one, it may not work as expected.

And if the data used to train that tool does not reflect the people it is meant to serve, the results can be biased.

But this is not only a technical issue. It is also about ownership, governance, and power.

Too often, health data is collected in low-resource settings, while the tools used to analyze it are built elsewhere. Those tools are then introduced back into the same settings without enough adaptation to local realities. When that happens, countries can become dependent on systems they do not fully control, and health workers may end up spending more time feeding systems than benefiting from them.

[Pause]

So who is most affected?

Patients in underserved communities may receive less accurate recommendations if AI tools are not built with their context in mind.

Healthcare workers may experience more burden if digital tools do not fit real clinical workflows.

And ministries of health may lose ownership when systems are driven more by external priorities than national needs.

So yes, AI can widen existing gaps.

But that is not inevitable.

AI is not automatically good or bad. It reflects the choices we make in how we design it, govern it, and use it.

If we make better choices, AI can help strengthen health systems instead of deepening inequities.

What does that require?

First, local co-design. Health workers, ministries, researchers, and communities need to be involved from the beginning.

Second, stronger national data systems. AI works best when digital systems are interoperable, secure, and locally governed.

Third, investment in local expertise. Countries need local engineers, data scientists, analysts, and public health professionals who can build and manage these systems themselves.

Fourth, transparency. If AI is used in healthcare, people should understand how it works, what data it uses, and what risks it carries.

And finally, AI should solve real health system problems. It should help detect outbreaks earlier, identify treatment interruptions, improve forecasting, and strengthen data quality. It should support care, not just reporting.

[Pause]

The future we need is not one where AI replaces health workers.

It is one where AI supports them.

It is one where countries have stronger digital systems, stronger ownership, and stronger local innovation.

Artificial intelligence has the potential to transform healthcare. But whether it reduces inequity or reinforces it will depend on the choices we make now.

If we build AI with equity, transparency, and local leadership at the center, it can become a powerful tool for stronger health systems.

If we do not, it risks becoming just another layer of inequality.

Thank you for listening.

[Music fades out]
