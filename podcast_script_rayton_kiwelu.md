# Podcast Script

**Title:** Beyond Dashboards: How AI Can Strengthen or Worsen Global Health Inequities

[Opening music fades in]

Hello, and welcome.

My name is Rayton Kiwelu. I am a database developer and digital health professional, and much of my work focuses on health systems in resource-limited settings. I work with large health datasets, health information system integration, and digital tools that support care and treatment programs serving thousands of patients.

Over the years, I have seen how digital technology can improve healthcare. But I have also seen how badly designed systems can create more work, more confusion, and sometimes even more inequality.

Today, I want to talk about artificial intelligence, or AI, and an important question that comes with it:

How can we use AI in healthcare without making global health inequities even worse?

[Pause]

AI is becoming part of healthcare very quickly.

We are seeing tools that can support diagnosis, analyze medical images, predict outbreaks, improve supply chains, and help health workers make faster decisions. On paper, this sounds promising, and in many ways, it is.

AI has real potential to strengthen health systems.

But that is only part of the story.

Most AI tools used in healthcare today are designed and trained in high-income countries. They are often built using data from hospitals with strong infrastructure, reliable internet, stable electricity, and well-connected digital systems.

That is not the reality in many low- and middle-income countries.

In many settings, health workers are already dealing with limited resources, high patient volumes, unstable connectivity, and multiple systems that do not communicate well with each other. So when an AI tool is developed in one environment and then dropped into a very different one, it may not perform well. In some cases, it may create new problems instead of solving existing ones.

One major concern is data bias.

If an AI system is trained on populations that do not reflect the people it is meant to serve, its recommendations may be less accurate, less useful, or even harmful. This is especially serious in healthcare, where decisions affect real lives.

But the challenge is not only technical. It is also about power, ownership, and governance.

Too often, global health follows a familiar pattern: data is collected in low-resource settings, the tools are built somewhere else, and then the solutions are brought back without enough adaptation to local realities.

This can reinforce dependency instead of building local capacity.

It can also mean that healthcare workers spend valuable time entering data into systems designed mainly for reporting, rather than systems that actually support patient care.

[Pause]

So who is most affected by this?

First, patients in rural and underserved communities. If an AI tool does not reflect their context, their needs, or their health profiles, they may not benefit from it equally.

Second, healthcare workers. A system that does not fit the daily workflow of a clinic can easily become an extra burden instead of a useful tool.

Third, ministries of health and national programs. If countries rely too heavily on external systems and external technical direction, national ownership of data and digital innovation becomes weaker.

And finally, communities with low digital access or low digital literacy can be left even further behind.

So yes, AI can widen inequalities.

But that is not inevitable.

AI is not automatically good or bad. It reflects the choices we make when we design it, fund it, deploy it, and govern it.

If we make better choices, AI can actually help strengthen health systems and reduce inequities.

[Pause]

So what do better choices look like?

First, local co-design.

AI tools should not be built for health systems without involving the people who actually use those systems. Frontline health workers, ministries of health, researchers, and communities all need to be part of the design process. They understand the real workflow, the real challenges, and the real priorities.

Second, stronger national data infrastructure.

Before AI can work well, countries need digital systems that are interoperable, secure, and locally governed. Data should move across systems in a way that supports real-time decision-making, not just reporting to external partners.

Third, investment in local expertise.

Countries need local data scientists, engineers, analysts, and public health professionals who can build, adapt, and manage these systems themselves. Without local expertise, AI will always remain something imported instead of something owned.

Fourth, transparency and accountability.

If AI is used in healthcare, people should be able to understand how decisions are made, what data is being used, and what risks exist. Health technologies should be explainable, ethical, and subject to oversight.

And finally, AI should focus on real health system problems.

Not hype. Not trends. Real problems.

AI could help identify disease outbreaks earlier. It could flag interruptions in treatment. It could improve commodity forecasting. It could help detect duplicate records or data quality issues in large databases. These are practical uses that can strengthen systems in meaningful ways.

[Pause]

If we imagine the future we want, it is not a future where AI replaces health workers.

It is a future where AI supports them.

It is a future where digital systems are connected, trusted, and designed around patient care.

It is a future where countries have ownership over their own data, their own infrastructure, and their own innovation.

And it is a future where equity is not an afterthought, but a starting point.

To get there, we need to change how global health technology is developed and financed. We need long-term investment instead of short-term pilots. We need partnerships that build national capacity instead of dependency. And we need to treat governance, context, and usability as seriously as we treat technical performance.

Artificial intelligence has the potential to transform healthcare.

But whether it reduces inequity or reinforces it will depend on the decisions we make now.

If we design AI with equity, transparency, and local leadership at the center, it can become a powerful tool for stronger health systems.

If we do not, it risks becoming just another layer of inequality.

[Closing pause]

Thank you for listening.

[Music fades out]
